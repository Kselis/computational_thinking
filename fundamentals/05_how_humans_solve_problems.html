How Humans Solve Problems: Our Amazing Brain Power

Hello there! I am so happy you are joining me today at the Kselis Institute. We are going to talk about something we all do every single day: solving problems. It might seem complicated, but solving problems is actually one of the most fascinating things about being human. We don't have a magic formula, but we have incredible tools in our brains that help us figure things out. Think of your brain as an amazing detective agency!

The Tools We Use to Find Solutions

When we face a problem—maybe you can’t open a jar, or you need to plan a big trip—we don't just sit and wait for the answer. Instead, our brains automatically start using several powerful methods to find a way through it. These methods are like different types of tools in a toolbox.

### Trial and Error: Trying It Out

Trial and error is one of the most basic ways we solve problems. How does it work? We guess a solution, try it out, see what happens, and if it doesn't work, we try something different. We keep repeating this process until we finally find something that works. The "error" part is actually very important because every wrong guess tells us one thing: "That path didn't work, so let's try a new direction."

### Observation: Paying Close Attention

Observation is like being a keen detective watching the world around you. When we observe, we notice details—what things look like, what people do, and what patterns exist. If you are trying to fix a leaky pipe, observing how water flows, where the leak is dripping from, and what materials are involved helps you see the problem clearly before you even start guessing solutions. We use observation to gather the raw facts about the situation.

### Experience: Learning from the Past

Experience is all the lessons we have learned from past successes and failures. If you’ve tried to bake a cake five times and it always comes out flat, your experience tells you that maybe you need more baking powder next time. Experience allows us to avoid repeating mistakes. It helps us predict outcomes based on what has happened before, making our future attempts smarter and faster.

### Intuition: The Inner Feeling

Intuition is that wonderful feeling or gut instinct we sometimes have. It’s not just guessing; it’s a fast, almost instant understanding that comes from all the information we have stored in our memories (our experience) and what we observe. It’s like your brain quickly taking all those past lessons and observations and making a smart guess about what might work next, even before you consciously think about it.

Analogy: Think about learning to ride a bicycle. At first, you just try to balance, fall over (trial and error), and feel wobbly. Then, you start observing how much you lean left or right, and your experience tells you that leaning slightly to the right helps you stay upright. Eventually, you develop an intuition—that feeling in your body that tells you exactly how much to shift your weight before you fall. Intuition is that quick, internal sense built from all your past attempts and observations.

The Strengths and Weaknesses of Human Problem Solving

So, we use trial and error, observation, experience, and intuition. These tools are incredibly powerful, but like any tool, they have both great strengths and some limitations. Understanding these helps us solve problems better!

### Strengths: Why Humans Are Great at It

The biggest strength of human problem-solving is our **creativity** combined with our ability to learn from mistakes. Because we can combine new ideas (creativity) with old knowledge (experience), we can invent brand new solutions that no one has ever thought of before. We can adapt quickly when a plan fails, which keeps us moving forward toward the solution. Our intuition also helps us make quick decisions when time is short.

### Weaknesses: Where We Can Struggle

However, these methods also have weaknesses. Sometimes, our experience can be misleading. If we only rely on past experiences, we might get stuck trying to use an old solution even when a new one is needed. Also, intuition can sometimes lead us down wrong paths if it isn't backed up by solid observation or facts. For example, if you feel strongly that Solution A is right, but you don't observe the actual results, you might miss a better option. We can also get stuck in "fixation," where we focus too much on one possible answer and ignore other possibilities.

In summary, solving problems is a mix of action (trial and error), awareness (observation), memory (experience), and instinct (intuition). By using all these tools thoughtfully—checking our intuition against what we have seen and learned—we become truly effective problem-solvers! Keep practicing these skills, and you will find that every challenge becomes an opportunity to learn something new.
