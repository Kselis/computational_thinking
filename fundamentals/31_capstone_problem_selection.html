Capstone: Selecting a Problem – Finding What to Solve

Hello there! Welcome to our session at the Kselis Institute. Today, we are diving into one of the most important steps in any project or innovation process: selecting a real-world problem. It might sound tricky, but I promise you, once you learn how to look at a situation clearly, it becomes much easier. Think of this as learning how to be a detective for your own life and the world around you!

Why We Must Define a Problem First

Before we can build anything—before we can design a solution—we have to understand exactly what we are trying to fix. If you try to solve something without clearly defining the problem, you end up solving the wrong thing, which wastes all your time and energy. Defining a problem is like drawing a map before starting a journey. It stops us from wandering around aimlessly. We need to move from a vague feeling ("Things are messy") to a specific statement ("We need a system to reduce food waste by 20%"). This clarity is the foundation of a successful project.

The Four Essential Pieces: Goals, Inputs, Outputs, and Constraints

To turn a general mess into a solvable challenge, we break the problem down into four simple, measurable parts. These four elements—Goals, Inputs, Outputs, and Constraints—tell us exactly what we need to achieve and what limits we have while doing it.

### Goals: What Do We Want to Achieve? Your goal is the desired end result. This is the "why" behind your project. Goals must be clear and measurable. If your goal is just "make things better," that’s too vague. A good goal is specific. For example, instead of "reduce waste," a better goal would be "reduce the amount of uneaten food in the refrigerator by 30% within one month." Goals give you direction; they tell you where you are going.

### Inputs: What Do We Need to Start? Inputs are all the resources, materials, information, or energy you need to begin the process. These are the ingredients for your solution. If your goal is to reduce food waste, your inputs might be things like a plan, a small storage container, knowledge about food expiration dates, and perhaps some time to organize. Inputs are what you gather before you start building.

### Outputs: What Will We Produce? Outputs are the tangible results or products that come directly from your process. This is what you create. If you successfully implement your plan, your output might be a new system for storing leftovers, a set of clear labels on food items, or a documented habit change in your household. The output is the actual thing you deliver.

### Constraints: What Limits Us? Constraints are the limitations you have to work within. These are the rules, limitations, or restrictions that make the task challenging. Constraints force you to be creative! For example, if your goal is to reduce food waste, a constraint might be: "We only have a small kitchen budget," or "We can only use reusable containers," or "We only have one hour per week to manage this." Constraints tell you what you *cannot* do, which helps you find clever ways around those limits.

Analogy: Think of planning a road trip. Your **Goal** is to get from New York to Los Angeles. Your **Inputs** are your car, gas, food, and a map. Your **Outputs** are arriving safely in Los Angeles with a full tank of gas. Your **Constraints** are the distance you have to travel, the time you have available, and the fuel limits of your car. If you ignore the constraints (like running out of gas), you won't reach your goal!

Putting It All Together: A Simple Example

Let’s see how this works when we apply it to a simple, real-world problem. Let’s choose the problem: **"Managing and reducing food waste in a small apartment kitchen."**

1. **Goal:** To reduce the amount of edible food thrown away in the kitchen by 25% over the next two weeks. (This is specific and measurable.) 2. **Inputs:** Knowledge about food storage techniques, reusable containers (like glass or sturdy plastic), a weekly meal planning schedule, and time set aside for food prep. 3. **Outputs:** A new system of labeled, organized food storage containers; a documented weekly meal plan; and a reduction in the amount of spoiled food waste. 4. **Constraints:** The budget must remain under \$20 for new supplies; the solution must be achievable with less than one hour of extra work per week; and all solutions must use items already available in the kitchen.

See how much clearer this is? We started with a vague idea, and by breaking it down into these four parts, we now have a clear roadmap. We know exactly what we want (Goal), what we need to start (Inputs), what we will create (Outputs), and what rules we must follow (Constraints). This structure ensures that whatever solution we create is actually practical and achievable! Keep practicing this method, and you will become an expert problem-solver!
