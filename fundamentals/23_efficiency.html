Understanding Efficiency: Choosing the Smartest Way to Solve Things

Hello there! I’m so happy you are joining me at the Kselis Institute today. We are going to talk about something very important in life, whether you are solving a math problem, planning a project, or just trying to get through your day: **Efficiency**.

When we talk about efficiency, we aren't just talking about being fast. True efficiency is about getting the most good result possible while using the least amount of things we need—like time, energy, and materials. Think of it like this: if you want to bake a cake, you can use three different recipes. One recipe might be super fast but uses too much expensive flour (poor resource use). Another might take a long time but uses very little ingredients (high efficiency in resources). We need to figure out which path is the *best* path for our specific goal.

The Three Ingredients of Efficiency: Time, Effort, and Resources

To decide which solution is better, we need to look at three main ingredients. These are the things we spend when trying to achieve something.

### Time: How Fast Can We Finish?

Time is often the first thing we think about. A solution that saves time seems immediately appealing, right? However, simply being the fastest isn't always the most efficient choice. We need to ask *why* it is fast. Is it fast because we skipped necessary steps, or is it fast because we used a highly specialized tool that saved hours of manual labor?

The goal with time is not just speed; it’s about finding the optimal balance. Sometimes, taking a little extra time allows us to avoid mistakes later on, which saves even more time overall. We look for solutions where the time spent directly relates to the quality of the outcome.

### Effort: How Much Hard Work Is Required?

Effort is the energy and mental focus you put into a task. A highly efficient solution minimizes wasted effort. If a solution requires massive, exhausting effort just to get a small result, it is inefficient, even if it finishes quickly. We want solutions that use our energy wisely. This means choosing methods that fit our natural strengths and avoid unnecessary struggle.

### Resources: What Do We Need to Use?

Resources are the materials, money, tools, and energy we consume. This is where efficiency really shines. A solution is efficient if it achieves the desired outcome using the minimum amount of these resources. For example, if you are building a shelf, an inefficient solution might involve buying expensive custom-cut wood (high resource cost), whereas an efficient solution might use readily available scrap materials and simple tools (low resource cost).

Putting It All Together: Comparing Solutions

So, how do we decide which solution is truly better? We compare the solutions based on how they handle these three ingredients together. A solution that wins is the one that provides the best outcome for the least amount of input across time, effort, and resources.

Let’s look at an example to see this in action: Imagine you need to move a heavy box across the room.

Analogy: Imagine you are trying to cross a large river. Solution A is building a long, sturdy bridge. This takes a lot of time and uses a lot of resources (wood, labor). Solution B is swimming across the river. This takes less time initially, but requires immense physical effort (high effort) and risks exhaustion if the water is strong (high risk/effort). Solution C is using a small, lightweight raft that you push across. This might take slightly longer in total time than the bridge, but it requires very little material (low resource use) and moderate effort. In this case, Solution C is often the most *efficient* because it balances the need for completion with minimal use of materials and energy.

See how the analogy shows that just being the fastest (like swimming quickly) isn't always the best choice if you consider the overall cost of effort and resources? The bridge might be faster, but the raft is more efficient because it manages the limits of what you have available.

When evaluating any solution, ask yourself these three questions: 1. **Time:** How long will this take compared to other options? 2. **Effort:** How much physical or mental energy will I need to put in? 3. **Resources:** How much money, material, or energy will this consume?

The most efficient solution is the one that gives you a great result without draining your time, exhausting your effort, or wasting your resources. By thinking about all three things together, you move from just finding *a* solution to finding the *best* solution! Keep practicing this way of thinking, and you will become very skilled at making smart choices.
