Understanding Computational Thinking (CT) vs. Programming

The most important thing we need to grasp right away is the difference between two big ideas: Computational Thinking and Programming. These terms are often used together, but they mean different things.

Computational Thinking (CT) is like learning *how* to think about a problem in a way that a computer can understand. It is a set of problem-solving skills. When you use computational thinking, you learn how to break down a huge, scary problem into smaller, manageable steps. You learn to look for patterns, figure out the best order to tackle things, and create logical steps to reach a solution. CT is the *thinking* part—it’s the strategy.

Programming, on the other hand, is the act of writing those specific instructions down using a particular language (like Python or JavaScript) so that a machine can actually follow them and perform a task. Programming is the *doing* part—it’s the actual building of the solution using code. You need computational thinking first to know *what* steps to take, and then you use programming to write the exact instructions for the computer to follow those steps.

Analogy: Imagine you want to bake a complicated cake. Computational Thinking is like planning the entire baking process: figuring out the ingredients needed, deciding the order of steps (preheat oven first, mix dry ingredients next, add wet ingredients later), and predicting what might go wrong if you skip a step. Programming is like actually reading the recipe card and carefully following every single instruction written on it to make the cake.

So, to summarize: CT is the brain skill (the strategy for solving); Programming is the tool skill (writing the specific instructions). We will focus heavily on building that strong thinking foundation first!

Course Structure and Learning Goals

This course is designed to take you from a general problem-solver to someone who can logically approach challenges using computational methods. Our main goal is not just to teach you how to write code, but to teach you *how to think* like a programmer.

Our structure will move step-by-step. We will start with simple puzzles and logic games to build our thinking muscles. Then, we will introduce basic concepts of algorithms (which are just step-by-step instructions), data representation, and finally, we will apply these ideas by writing actual programs. By the end of this course, you will be able to look at any complex problem—whether it’s organizing a schedule, finding the best route, or sorting a list—and automatically think in a logical, computational way about how to solve it.

Assessments and Expectations

To make sure we are all learning effectively, we will have several ways of checking your understanding. Our assessments will include weekly coding challenges where you apply the thinking skills we learn, short quizzes on concepts like loops and conditionals (the building blocks of programming), and a final project where you use everything you’ve learned to solve a larger, real-world problem.

My expectation for you is active participation and persistence. Don't be afraid to make mistakes! Mistakes are not failures; they are just opportunities to learn what doesn't work and try a new approach. I expect you to come to class ready to try things out, ask questions when you are stuck, and work hard on your logic. We are here to explore, experiment, and grow together as we learn this powerful way of solving problems!
