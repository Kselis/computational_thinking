Understanding Feedback Loops: How Things Keep Changing

Hello there! I’m so happy you are joining me at the Kselis Institute. Today we are going to explore a really cool idea in science and everyday life called **Feedback Loops**. Don't let the big words scare you; this is actually how almost everything around us works—from the air conditioning in your room to what you see on your phone!

What Exactly is a Feedback Loop?

Imagine a system as a circle or a loop. A feedback loop is simply a situation where the *output* (the result you get) of a process goes back and becomes the *input* (what you use to start the next step). It’s a cycle of cause and effect that keeps things moving, changing, or staying the same.

Think of it like a conversation: you say something (that's an action), the other person reacts (that's the output), and their reaction changes what you say next (that’s the new input). In a feedback loop, this cycle repeats over and over again. The key is that the result always influences the start of the next step.

How Feedback Loops Work: The Two Main Types

Feedback loops generally fall into two main types, depending on whether they try to push things further apart or bring them back to balance.

First, we have **Balancing Loops**. These loops are designed to keep a system stable and balanced. If something gets too high or too low, the loop kicks in to correct it and bring it back to a comfortable middle ground. This is like a thermostat trying to maintain a perfect temperature.

Second, we have **Amplifying Loops**. These loops cause things to grow bigger, faster, or change more dramatically over time. If you start with a small change, an amplifying loop makes that change grow exponentially. This is often seen in situations where things accelerate.

Analogy: Think about trying to walk on a rubber ball. When you push down (input), the ball moves (output). If you stop pushing, it rolls back to its original spot. That’s a simple cycle. A feedback loop is like when you are walking up a hill: your effort (input) makes you move forward (output), and that forward movement tells your brain you need to keep walking harder (new input). This keeps the process going!

Real-Life Example 1: The Thermostat (Balancing Loop)

Let’s look at a perfect example of a balancing loop: a thermostat.

**The Situation:** You want your room to be exactly 70 degrees.

**How it Works (The Cycle):** 1. **The Measurement (Input):** The thermometer measures the current room temperature. Let's say it is currently 75 degrees (too hot). 2. **The Action (Output):** Since 75 is higher than the desired 70, the thermostat sends a signal to turn the air conditioner ON. 3. **The Result (New Input):** The AC runs and starts cooling the room. The temperature starts dropping. 4. **The Check (Feedback):** The thermometer measures the new temperature. It sees that it is now 72 degrees. Since 72 is still higher than 70, the thermostat keeps sending the signal to the AC to keep running until the temperature hits exactly 70 degrees.

See how the output (the current temperature) immediately feeds back into the system to adjust the input (how much cooling is needed)? This constant adjustment ensures the room stays at the desired setting. It balances itself out!

Real-Life Example 2: Social Media Recommendations (Amplifying Loop)

Now let’s look at how feedback loops can cause things to grow, which is an amplifying loop. This happens all the time with social media algorithms.

**The Situation:** The goal of the social media platform is to keep you engaged and scrolling.

**How it Works (The Cycle):** 1. **Initial Action (Input):** You open the app and click on a post about cats. 2. **The System Reacts (Output):** The algorithm sees your interest in cats and shows you more cat videos, posts, and articles. 3. **Your Reaction (New Input):** Because you spent time watching those cat things, you stop scrolling and spend more time engaging with the cat content. You click on three more cat posts. 4. **The Amplification (Feedback):** The algorithm sees that you are spending more time looking at cat content, so it feeds you even *more* cat content next.

This loop amplifies your attention. The initial action led to a result, and that result caused you to take another action, which caused the system to push you further down that path. If this cycle is strong enough, it can lead to you spending much more time on the app than you intended!

Understanding feedback loops helps us see how cause and effect are constantly interacting in the world around us. Keep observing these cycles—you’ll start seeing them everywhere!
